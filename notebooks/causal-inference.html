Causal Inference

When you have multiple variables like \(X, Y, \text{and } Z\), and you want to know how one effects the others, causally.

For example:

Does eating a Mediterranean-style diet lower your risk of heart disease?

Does smoking cause cancer?

And several other, perhaps more controversial, examples.

  • Big Picture:

    • D. Mackenzie and J. Pearl. The Book of Why: The New Science of Cause and Effect. 2018. link

    • Cosma Shalizi's chapters on causal inference in Advanced Data Science from an Elementary Point of View (Part III).

  • The Impossibility of Causal Discovery

    • J. M. Robins, R. Scheines, P. Spirtes, and L. Wasserman, "Uniform consistency in causal inference," Biometrika, vol. 3, no. 90, pp. 491–515, 2003.
      • The short version: within a certain class of models1,
        1. universal consistency is achieved.
        2. uniform consistency is not achievable when time order is unknown and / or unobservable confounders are present.

  • Some Hope for Causal Discovery:

    • J. Zhang and P. Spirtes, "Strong faithfulness and uniform consistency in causal inference," Proceedings of the Nineteenth conference on Uncertainty in Artificial Intelligence, 2002, pp. 632–639.
      • Under stricter assumptions, the causal discovery algorithms are uniformly consistent. As the authors put it, 'Uniform Consistency with More Faithfulness'.

  1. The assumptions that the data generating distribution is (1) Markov to the causal graph and (2) faithful to the causal graph. ↩
