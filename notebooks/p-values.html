Some Common Errors and Mis-interpretations:

  1. Interpretting $P_{\theta_{0}}(|T| \geq |T^{}|)$ as $P(H_{0} \mid |T| = |T^{}|)$, a.k.a. interpretting the $P$-value as the probability of the null hypothesis given the data1.

  2. Inpretting $P_{\theta_{0}}(|T| \geq |T^{*}|)$ as the probability that the result occurred due to random chance. This is basically the same error as Error 1, but in even looser language.

  3. Interpretting $P_{\theta_{0}}(|T| \geq |T^{*}|)$ as the probability of making a False Positive Error, i.e. observing $p = 0.023$ does not mean you have a 2.3% chance of falsely rejecting the null. This is what the level / size / "$\alpha$" of the test controls: if you fix $\alpha$ at, say, 0.05, then under the null, you would incorrectly reject the null at most $100\alpha\%$ of the time.

  4. Interpretting a small $P$-value as a strong effect. A small $P$-value indicates that (a) you have a precisely measured effect that is different from the null effect or (b) you are very unlucky.

  5. Related to 3: interpretting statistical significance as practical significance.

  6. Using $P$-values from a regression to determine which predictor / covariate / 'independent variable' is most relevant to predicting an outcome / 'dependent variable'.

  1. This error is often made in both directions: a large $P$-value is interpretted as indicating strong evidence for the null hypothesis, and a small $P$-value is interpretted as indicating strong direct evidence for the alternative hypothesis. Neither is true.

    The error in the second direction requires additional clarification: a very small P-value is evidence against the null hypothesis. However, it is not direct evidence, in the sense of a direct interpretation of the $P$-value as $P(H_{0} \mid |T| = |T^{*}|)$. For example, the $P$-value is not generally equal to the evidence for the null hypothesis in a Bayesian framework. See:

    J. O. Berger, T. Sellke, 1987, "Testing a Point Null Hypothesis: The Irreconcilability of P Values and Evidence," Journal of the American Statistical Association, vol. 82, no. 397, pp. 112–122, Mar. 1987. ↩
