Study Guide for Exam 3

This will be a closed-book exam. You will be allowed to use R on a school computer for computations. This means you will not have access to, nor will you need to use, anything beyond Base R (e.g. mosaic, MUsaic, etc.) for the exam.

To do well on the exam, you should be able to do the following:

Chapter 15

How confidence intervals behave

  1. Distinguish between an interval estimator and an interval estimate (aka “confidence interval”).
  2. Explain what the probability \(c\) is associated with for a confidence level \(c\) confidence interval.
  3. Identify how the width of a confidence interval varies as \(n\), \(c\), and \(s_{X}\) vary.

Chapter 17

The \(t\) distributions

  1. State the sampling distribution of a \(Z\)-score for a sample mean from a Normal population.
  2. State the sampling distribution of a \(T\)-score for a sample mean from a Normal population.
  3. Identify the degrees of freedom for the \(t\) distribution resulting from a \(T\)-score for a sample mean.
  4. Compare and contrast the density curve for a \(t\)-distributed random variable to the density curve of a standard Normal random variable.
  5. Identify how the shape of the density curve of a \(t\)-distributed random variable changes as its degrees of freedom increase.
  6. Compute a critical value \(t_{\alpha, n-1}\) for a \(t\)-distribution with \(n - 1\) degrees of freedom using qt in R.

The one-sample \(t\) confidence interval

  1. Compute a \(t\)-based confidence interval given relevant information about a random sample from a Normal population.
  2. Sketch how the \(t\)-based confidence interval for a population mean is related to the sample mean, critical value, and estimate of the standard error of the sample mean.

Chapter 14

Hypothesis testing

  1. Identify what types of quantities a statistical hypothesis always addresses.
  2. Identify the population parameter from a statistical claim.
  3. Given a claim about a population parameter \(\theta\) as a “word problem,” write the claim as \(\theta \, \, \square \, \, \theta_{0}\) where the “box” \(\square\) contains one of \(=, \neq, \leq, \geq, <,\) or \(>\) and \(\theta_{0}\) is the claimed value of the population parameter.
  4. Define null hypothesis and alternative hypothesis.
  5. Given a claim in the form \(\theta \, \, \square \, \, \theta_{0}\), identify whether the claim is a null hypothesis or an alternative hypothesis.
  6. Given a claim in the form \(\theta \, \, \square \, \, \theta_{0}\), identify the associated null and alternative hypotheses for the claim.
  7. Recognize the symbols \(H_{0}\) (“H-naught”) and \(H_{a}\) (“H-a”) as denoting the null and alternative hypotheses, respectively.
  8. Explain why it never (no really, never) makes sense to frame a statistical hypothesis in terms of a sample statistic.

Tests for a population mean

  1. Given a claim about a population mean \(\mu_{X}\) as a “word problem,” write the claim as \(\mu_{X} \, \, \square \, \, \mu_{0}\) where the “box” \(\square\) contains one of \(=, \neq, \leq, \geq, <,\) or \(>\) and \(\mu_{0}\) is the claimed value of the population mean.
  2. Given a claim about a population mean \(\mu_{X}\) as a “word problem,” determine the corresponding null and alternative hypotheses.
  3. State a reasonable test statistic for testing a claim about a population mean.

Chapter 14

  2. Compute the observed \(T\)-score, denoted \(t_{\text{obs}}\), from a “word problem” about a population mean.
  3. Given an observed sample mean, sample standard deviation, and sample size, and a pair of null / alternative hypotheses, determine whether the observed sample mean provides evidence against the null hypothesis (equivalently, for the alternative hypothesis).
  4. State the sampling distribution of the \(T\)-score under the null hypothesis when the population distribution is Normal (or approximately so).

Chapter 14

\(P\)-value and statistical significance

  1. State how a \(P\)-value is defined for an observed test statistic and a given null hypothesis.
  2. State the rejection rule for using the \(P\)-value of an observed test statistic to reject (or not) the null hypothesis at a given significance level \(\alpha\).

Chapter 15

How hypothesis tests behave

  1. Define the two types of errors one can make in a hypothesis test, and state their names.
  2. Relate the two types of errors that one can make in a hypothesis test to decisions in legal trials and during warfare.
  3. Define the two error rates of a hypothesis test.
  4. Recognize \(\alpha\) as indicating the Type I Error Rate of a hypothesis test.
  5. Recognize the synonym of “significance level” for the Type I Error Rate \(\alpha\).
  6. State which of the two error rates are explicitly controlled for in constructing a hypothesis test.
  7. Define test statistic, and explain the distinction between a test statistic and an observed test statistic.
  8. Define the rejection region of a hypothesis test, and state how the rejection region is used to make a conclusion about a null hypothesis.
  9. State the 7 step procedure for testing a claim about a population using a hypothesis test that controls the Type I Error Rate.

Chapter 17

  2. State the left-sided, right-sided, and two-sided rejection regions for the one-sample \(t\) test.
  3. Identify when a left-sided, right-sided, or two-sided rejection region is appropriate to test a given null hypothesis.
  4. Construct a left-sided, right-sided, or two-sided rejection region given a claim about a population and a significance level \(\alpha\).
  5. Determine whether an observed test statistic “falls in” a rejection region.
  6. Determine the appropriate tail of the \(t\)-distribution to use to compute a \(P\)-value for a given null hypothesis.
  7. Compute the \(P\)-value for an observed \(t\)-statistic, and use the \(P\)-value to conclude whether or not to reject a given null hypothesis.
